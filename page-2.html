New York-style pizza has its roots in Neapolitan pizza from Naples, Italy. When Italian immigrants came to the U.S. in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, they brought their pizza-making traditions with them. The first known pizzeria in the U.S. was Lombardi’s, opened in 1905 in Manhattan’s Little Italy by Gennaro Lombardi. He adapted traditional Neapolitan pizza to American tastes and cooking methods—using a coal-fired oven instead of a wood-fired one, and making larger slices that could be eaten on the go.By the 1920s–30s, pizza was spreading through the five boroughs and developing its own identity:

  • Large
    , thin, foldable slices.
  • Crispy yet chewy crust, due to high-gluten bread flour and NYC’s mineral-rich tap water
  • Simple toppings: Tomato sauce and mozzarella were standard, with optional extras like pepperoni or mushrooms
  • Baked in coal or gas ovens, giving a slightly charred, flavorful crust.
